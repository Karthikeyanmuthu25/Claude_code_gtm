## 🎣 Top Hook Patterns Working Right Now

1. **Mistake/Reframe Hook**: *"Most founders build their GTM for a customer who doesn't exist yet."*
   - Why it works: Names a specific, believable mistake before the reader can object — creates instant self-identification. High comment rate because readers either agree (share their story) or push back (defend their approach). Both are engagement wins.
   - Engagement pattern: triggers "that's me" or "actually, here's why you're wrong" — both responses feed the algorithm.

2. **Contrarian Reframe Hook**: *"Your ICP isn't the problem. Building GTM before you know your Early Customer Persona is."*
   - Why it works: Challenges a widely-held belief (ICP = the foundation) without dismissing it entirely — feels nuanced, not clickbait. Stops scroll for ICP-familiar audiences (i.e., your exact ICP) who assume they've solved this.
   - Engagement pattern: High saves (framework-forward), strong comment thread from PMMs and GTM leads.

3. **Tension/Stakes Hook**: *"The persona that gets you to $1M ARR will stall you at $3M. Here's why."*
   - Why it works: Attaches a financial outcome to the insight — makes the cost of the mistake concrete and personal. Founders are hyper-attuned to ARR milestones. Creates urgency to read.
   - Engagement pattern: High reshare rate; founders tag co-founders and GTM hires in comments.

4. **Authority Observation Hook**: *"After seeing 50+ early-stage GTM motions, one mistake shows up more than any other: conflating Early Customer Persona with ICP."*
   - Why it works: Establishes credibility via pattern recognition, not credentials. Feels practitioner-first, not academic. The number ("50+") adds specificity and credibility without bragging.
   - Engagement pattern: Strong comment quality (longer, more substantive responses) — high Depth Score signals.

5. **Question Hook**: *"Do you know who actually bought your first 10 deals — and why they're probably not your ICP?"*
   - Why it works: Forces the reader to mentally audit their own situation before answering. Best used on posts targeting problem-unaware founders. High "See more" click rate due to curiosity gap.
   - Engagement pattern: High comment rate from founders at early traction stage; invite-to-share format.

> ⚠️ **Hook caution**: Generic contrarian hooks ("Everyone is doing X wrong") are now flagged as engagement bait by LinkedIn's 2026 algorithm if they lack specificity. Hooks must point to a specific mistake, audience, or outcome to avoid reach suppression. Specificity is the differentiator.

## 🌡️ Topic Saturation Level

- **Status**: **Underserved** (with Evergreen foundation)

- **Reasoning**:
  - The general **ICP topic** is moderately saturated — HubSpot, dozens of GTM influencers, and SaaS content mills publish ICP templates constantly. These posts receive surface engagement but low comment depth, suggesting audiences are fatigued with generic takes.
  - The **Early Customer Persona vs ICP distinction** is a genuine content gap. Current LinkedIn and broader GTM content either conflates ECP with ICP entirely, or treats "narrow ICP" as the early-stage answer — missing the conceptual and strategic distinction between the two artifacts.
  - April Dunford, Lenny Rachitsky, and Kyle Poyar all touch on early-stage targeting and positioning — but none have staked a clear "ECP ≠ ICP" framework as a distinct, named concept. This is an ownable angle.
  - The topic has **evergreen relevance** (every new B2B SaaS company goes through this) plus **timely urgency** (AI-accelerated GTM motions in 2025–2026 amplify the cost of persona confusion — founders are shipping GTM sequences without foundational persona clarity).

- **Differentiation opportunity**:
  - Name the artifact explicitly: "Early Customer Persona" as a distinct term and tool, not just "narrow ICP"
  - Lead with the GTM *consequence* of conflation (messaging drift, wrong channel bets, stalled pipeline) — not the conceptual definition
  - Use the $1M → $10M ARR transition as the proof frame — this is the moment founders feel the persona mismatch most acutely
  - Avoid the template trap: do not offer a downloadable ICP worksheet. Offer a *mental model shift* instead. The audience is tired of templates.

## 📐 Recommended Post Structure for This Topic

```
[Line 1 — Hook: Name the specific mistake or tension]
[Line 2 — Agitate: The cost or consequence of that mistake]

[Line 3 — Reframe: Introduce the ECP vs ICP distinction as the insight]
[Line 4 — Define: One-line plain-English definition of each]

[Lines 5–7 — Framework: 3 concrete differences (ECP vs ICP) in scannable format]

[Lines 8–9 — Proof/Stakes: The $1M → $10M ARR transition as concrete proof]

[Line 10 — Resolution: What knowing your ECP unlocks for GTM]
[Line 11 — CTA: Question prompt that invites a thoughtful response]
```

**Example post skeleton (Variant A — Mistake Hook):**
```
Most founders build GTM for a customer who doesn't exist yet.

They write their ICP. They build sequences. They pick channels.
And then wonder why pipeline is soft.

The problem isn't execution. It's that they skipped a step:
defining the Early Customer Persona (ECP) before the ICP.

These are not the same thing:

→ ECP: Who actually buys first. Real buyers, real context, real constraints.
→ ICP: Who you want to sell to at scale. Aspirational. Built for growth.

Building GTM from your ICP when you only have 5 customers
means you're messaging to a persona built on assumptions.

The ECP gives you the real signal:
the exact language, triggers, and objections of your first 10–50 buyers.

Get that right before you build the system.

Who is your GTM actually built around — your early buyers or your future ones?
```

## 📝 Notes for linkedin-writer

- **Optimal post length**: 150–250 words for text posts. Carousel: 8–12 slides at 30–50 words per slide. Avoid long-form wall-of-text — this founder audience bails after 3 un-broken paragraphs.
- **Best day/time to post**: Tuesday or Wednesday, 7:30–9:00am in the author's local timezone. Engagement velocity in the first 60–90 minutes determines algorithmic reach — author must be available to reply to comments immediately after posting.
- **Early engagement play**: Author should reply to every comment within the first 2 hours. Seed the comment thread with a follow-up question from the author to extend the conversation and signal depth.
- **Hashtags with traction**: `#GTM` `#B2BSaaS` `#GoToMarket` `#SaaS` `#StartupGTM` `#FounderLed` — use 3–5 max. Do NOT stack hashtags at the bottom of the post; weave 1–2 into the closing naturally. Heavy hashtag stacking signals low-quality content to the 2026 algorithm.
- **Emojis**: Use sparingly — 1–2 per post maximum. Arrow (→) works well as a visual separator in framework lists. Avoid emojis in the hook line — they undercut the practitioner tone.
- **Carousel variant**: Lead with a cover slide that mirrors the hook (e.g., "Your ECP ≠ Your ICP — and the difference is stalling your GTM") and close with a slide that restates the CTA and follow prompt. 10 slides is the sweet spot for dwell time without drop-off.
- **Link handling**: Do NOT include external links in the post caption — LinkedIn's 2026 algorithm suppresses reach on posts with outbound links by up to 60%. Drop the blog link in the first comment instead, posted immediately after publishing.
- **Tone calibration**: Practitioner-first, not consultant-speak. Write as a founder who has witnessed this mistake — not as an analyst who studied it. First-person preferred for Variant A (mistake hook). Third-person/observation frame works for Variant B or C (authority or data-led hooks).
- **Algorithm flag**: Avoid generic engagement bait ("Tag a founder who needs this"). Instead, close with a specific, substantive question that invites a genuine response — this drives comment quality, which carries 7–9x more algorithmic weight than reactions.
